% --- ABSTRACT ---
\begin{abstract}

    \noindent
    Accurate dietary assessment is essential for managing chronic conditions in older adults, yet clinical gold standards such as the USDA Automated Multiple-Pass Method impose cognitive and physical burdens that preclude longitudinal use. Conversely, passive computer-vision approaches reduce friction but sacrifice the clinical fidelity required when hidden variables---preparation methods, cooking oils, portion ambiguities---determine nutritional outcomes. This tension constitutes the \emph{friction-fidelity paradox} addressed by this thesis.

    The research follows a two-phase design. \textbf{Phase~1} employs a PRISMA-ScR scoping review to map the translational evidence on AI applications in geriatric nutrition. Analysis of 25 eligible studies reveals a nascent field: $92\%$ of systems remain at prototype or pilot stages, $84\%$ rely on private datasets, and a systematic mismatch persists between system design and the needs of older adults.

    \textbf{Phase~2} applies the Design Science Research Methodology to design, develop, and evaluate \emph{Snap and Say}, a multimodal agentic AI system that operationalises threshold-based routing to resolve the paradox. The artifact's core architecture comprises (1)~a Deterministic Complexity Scoring Engine that computes an auditable, non-linear complexity score from LLM-derived ambiguity levels and domain-curated food class metadata; (2)~a Clinical Threshold Routing Module that compares this score against a configurable clinical threshold; and (3)~a truncated adaptation of the AMPM, bounded by a safety budget of two clarification rounds.

    Simulated benchmarking ($N=500$ items) demonstrates that the agentic clarification cycles reduce caloric estimation mean absolute error by $30.6\%$ on complex items relative to a single-shot baseline, with a $92\%$ True Negative Rate on simple items confirming that the routing architecture successfully withholds intervention where it would impose burden without commensurate clinical value. A human-in-the-loop evaluation with older adults ($N=19$, Age $\geq 65$) confirms low perceived complexity and sustained willingness to engage with the multimodal interface.

    Together, the two phases demonstrate that the depth of conversational inquiry can be calibrated to the clinical stakes of each meal entry---asking more when the data demand it, and asking nothing when they do not.

    \bigskip
    \noindent\textbf{Keywords:} Agentic AI, Dietary Assessment, Geriatric Nutrition, Design Science Research, Scoping Review, Multimodal Interaction, Human-Computer Interaction

\end{abstract}
